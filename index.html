Research vision

Learning by imitation is central to behaviors like speech and music, but we still know surprisingly little about the underlying neural mechanisms. My research uses zebra finch song learning — a natural behavior in which young birds learn to copy the song of an adult tutor — to uncover how complex skills are learned. Songbirds are an especially powerful model because vocal imitation learning is exceptionally rare in the animal kingdom and they possess dedicated neural circuits for both producing and learning song, making it experimentally tractable to isolate the neural mechanisms of a behavior with key parallels to some of our most "human" behaviors.

The promise of birdsong has long been limited by two barriers: quantifying learning in high-dimensional vocal behavior and gaining precise access to the neural circuits that drive it. By combining machine-learning analyses of millions of vocalizations with circuit-specific perturbation and imaging in the singing bird, I overcame these challenges. In doing so, I identified a synaptic locus of song learning and revealed the circuit logic of learning at these cortico-basal ganglia synapses. I will build on this entry point to determine how molecular signals, synaptic plasticity, circuit dynamics, biological constraints, and computation transform practice into lasting motor memories.
